Noise in a Block

This is a Census Block:

We publish census data grouped by these areas, called blocks. Blocks can be empty, have a single house, or can have hundreds of residents, and are typically divided by geographic features like roads or creeks.

This block is home to a family of four, and this is what their data look like:

NameSexAge
MotherF43
FatherM42
DaughterF15
SonM12

The first thing we do is remove the names and other identifying information from the data:

NameSexAge
F43
M42
F15
M12

This may seem like enough to protect this family’s data, but what if someone knows this family’s address? What if someone knows the ages of the family's parents and is trying to learn the ages of the children? Just from the information above, one could easily re-identify the family's data, in violation of the Census Bureau's pledge of confidentiality.

To truly protect the confidentiality of family's data, we must do more. In order to provide useful data for statistical purposes while providing confidentiality to those who provide their data, we employ mathematical process called differential privacy that results in small, random changes to the data that we publish our reports.

This is called adding noise to the data, or noise injection.

For example, after the privacy mechanism runs, the data associated with this block might look like this:

Noise injection data creates uncertainty about the exact confidential data.

Say a person was looking at the data for this census block, and knew that only one family lived there. Knowing that noise has changed some of the data, that person would not know from our data whether the family has 0, 1, 2, 3, or even 4 female members. This uncertainty about which numbers are exact and which have been adjusted protects families from having their data identified.

Comparing the confidential and published statistics, the average age and Female Count have remained the same, but the Male Count has changed. The privacy mechanism used in the 2020 Census works to preserve the accuracy of specific statistics while protecting the confidentiality of the underlying data, as demonstrated here.

Although the population of this block changes as a result of the privacy mechanism, the privacy mechanism assures that these changes are not significant, especially when larger geographical areas are considered.
